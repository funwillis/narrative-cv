This document works as a quick guide so students can learn to look at human needs through the Jobs to Be Done framework: first understand its logic, then observe real examples, and finally place those jobs on a map of dimensions to detect opportunities.

People do not buy products. They hire them to make progress in a job.

1. The 3 dimensions of a job (framework foundations)

What must be understood before looking at examples

Functional

It is the practical task. What the person needs to solve in a concrete, visible, measurable way.

Question: what do I want to achieve or avoid?

Emotional

It is the inner state one wants to trigger or reduce: relief, calm, confidence, control, or motivation.

Question: how do I want to feel?

Social

It is the signal I send to others: belonging, status, competence, taste, identity, or reputation.

Question: how do I want to be perceived?

3. JTBD coordinates

Examples first, visual placement after
X axis: functional weight
Y axis: emotional load
Node size: social visibility

What the map means

Not all jobs weigh the same across the three dimensions. Some are highly practical, others highly emotional, and others highly social. The map helps that mixture become visible at a glance.

How to read it in class

  • Farther right: more practical task and concrete resolution.
  • Higher up: more relief, anxiety, bonding, or emotional identity.
  • Larger node: more social visibility or external signaling.

Useful question for students

If a profession disappeared tomorrow, what human job would still exist and with what new solution could it be solved again?

4. Full theory, briefly

Closure to consolidate the framework

Key concept

JTBD, popularized by Clayton Christensen, holds that people do not buy products because of their features, but because they help them make progress in a concrete situation.

  • Hire: choose a solution to do a job.
  • Fire: stop using it because it no longer solves that job.

Classic example

The McDonald’s milkshake did not compete only against other milkshakes. It also competed against donuts, bananas, or bagels in the job of “getting through the commute to work without hunger or complications”.

How to apply it

  • Identify the job: what the person is trying to achieve.
  • Define the context: when, where, and with what friction it happens.
  • Analyze alternatives: what solutions are hired today.
  • Design value: how to solve it better.
  • Validate: interview to understand the real why.

Why it helps

  • It helps innovation happen with meaning, not fashion.
  • It improves marketing by speaking about the real problem.
  • It segments by human tasks, not only demographic profiles.

Closing line: the important question is not “what product do people want?” but “what job do they need to solve?”
