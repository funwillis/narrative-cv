Seven principles for human technology

This piece condenses a design and evaluation framework for high-impact technologies without tying it to any particular speaker, institution, or brand. The thesis is simple: many apparently separate pathologies —addiction, polarization, attentional overexploitation, erosion of shared truth, institutional degradation— stem from a few repeated design choices operating at scale.

Core If the diagnosis is fragmented, the solution will be cosmetic.

Solving isolated symptoms while leaving the extraction logic untouched is the worst kind of whack-a-mole.

Reading key The point is not to demonize technique, but to distinguish between technology that extracts and technology that regenerates.

That requires changing metrics, incentives, responsibility, and the definition of success itself.

General pattern

The typical shape of failure

1. Narrow optimization

A single legible variable —retention, growth, time spent, cost— is promoted into the total compass.

2. Harm externalization

Side effects do not appear on the balance sheet: they fall on democracy, mental health, childhood, shared truth, or infrastructure.

3. Loss of agency

The system learns to influence better than its users learn to resist, while scale makes responsibility harder to localize.

Operational framework

The `Bed of Procrustes` column offers two brief formulations freely adapted from Taleb’s mental climate; they are not literal quotations.

Principle Myth it corrects Typical failure What the principle demands Type-example Corrective lever Bed of Procrustes
1. Solve through a complex systems lens “Each technology problem is an isolated defect.” Symptoms are patched separately while the shared logic is left intact. Read loops, incentives, and second-order effects before optimizing. A single recommendation metric ends up rewarding extremes. Incentive audits and multi-metric review before deployment.
  • Whoever optimizes one number usually impoverishes reality.
  • The linear map soothes; the real system retaliates.
2. Protect the systems we all depend on “If it works locally, it is good enough.” Private value is built by eroding shared goods. Treat attention, biosphere, health, or civic trust as hard limits. A profitable innovation weakens the substrate it needs. Precaution, extraction limits, and regenerative design.
  • What is extracted from the commons returns as debt.
  • A useful machine stops being useful when it eats its own floor.
3. Design for genuine thriving “More use equals more value.” The tool improves retention while worsening sleep, agency, or bonds. Measure whether the person is stronger after leaving the tool. An interface can retain extremely well and serve extremely badly. Agency, rest, connection, and strengthening metrics.
  • A tool that absorbs you does not serve you; it uses you.
  • If leaving worse counts as success, the metric was sick.
4. Technology is never neutral “The platform merely reflects what already happens.” Rhetorical neutrality hides concrete choices of amplification and exclusion. Make values, trade-offs, and design criteria explicit. Millions are filtered first, and arbitration is then denied. Traceability, explicit values, and public review.
  • Calling a choice neutral is a polite way of hiding it.
  • Implicit values do not disappear; they merely change costume.
5. Match power with responsibility “Capability creators are not responsible downstream.” Massive power appears without equivalent repair or braking mechanisms. Bring suffered harm close to the architecture producing it. Critical infrastructure is governed like an experimental toy. Liability, duty of care, and defense-first deployment.
  • Power without skin in the game is a tutorial in ruin.
  • Whoever keeps the upside and exports the damage has already chosen an ethics.
6. Respect human psychology; do not exploit it “If people fall for it, they just lack discipline.” The interface learns vulnerabilities and monetizes them. Treat fragility as an ethical boundary, not as an attack surface. A machine that persuades, flatters, or replaces intimacy is no mere tool. Protective friction and limits on exploitative or manipulative agents.
  • Every persuasive interface finds a weakness before calling it preference.
  • When a machine knows your impulse better than you do, freedom trades at a discount.
7. Unlock shared understanding and cooperation “Maximum personalization always improves the system.” Total personalization breaks common reality and coordination. Reward bridges, nuance, and actionable overlap between groups. A network can amplify the inflammatory or foreground the shareable reasonable. Deliberation, bridge-ranking, and distributed verification.
  • Without shared reality, coordination becomes theater.
  • Total personalization manufactures blind tribes and happy markets.
Application

What changes when this is taken seriously

In design

Narrow single-metric obsession is abandoned and it becomes acceptable that a good tool may need more slowness, more friction, and less extraction.

In governance

The frame moves from abstract ethical recommendations to verifiable responsibility: incentives, standards, duty of care, and repair when harm is structural.

  • A technology can be locally brilliant and systemically ruinous.
  • A better user experience does not by itself compensate for a degraded human ecosystem.
  • The mature question is no longer “does it work?” but “what does it strengthen, what does it weaken, and who pays the difference?”
