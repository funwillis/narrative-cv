Cosmos · interface · absurdity · adulthood

Holographic cosmos, interiority as interface

The thesis here is blunt and deliberately anti-plush: the cosmos may be a holographic reality and our interiority an interface. “Where am I?” Perhaps the question is already crooked. Light bounces, enters your eyes, and “you see me” in your brain. What absolutely distinguishes your mind from digital space, dream space, or the imaginary? Very little, except that we have learned to manipulate the macroscopic world with relative efficiency. That gives pragmatic power. It does not give epistemic closure.

Core We know very little. We navigate some layers of reality fairly well and mistake that for understanding it.

Mathematics and physics appear here not as “false,” but as tremendously powerful pragmatic tools unable to close the mystery of being.

Integration Camus brings the sobriety of the absurd; Krishnamurti freedom from fear; Perls psychic hygiene; Luri adult obligation.

The result is neither optimistic nor despairing: it is an ethic of hard lucidity, with few ornaments and quite a lot of responsibility.

Ontological thesis Reality may be more abstract than common sense tolerates.

What we call inside, outside, image, self, or world may be functional cuts rather than ultimate divisions of being.

Epistemic thesis Technical success does not equal final understanding.

Gödel enters here as an anti-triumphalist reminder: formal systems do not fully close upon themselves. Reason serves; it does not reign absolutely.

Existential thesis Life does not come with preloaded meaning.

The premise that it “must have one” may be an a priori bias. You may give it meaning, but there is no metaphysical obligation to find it written in the clouds.

Ethical thesis Absurdity does not mean everything goes.

Precisely because there is no cosmic guarantee, the adult must think better, act with less narcissism, and protect those who cannot yet bear the world.

Constellation

Camus, Krishnamurti, Perls, Luri

Camus

The absurd is not solved with comforting stories. It is inhabited. There is no guaranteed redemption, so living asks for sobriety without hysteria.

Krishnamurti

First and last freedom consists in looking without screens at the fact of death, social judgment, fear, and the desire for consolation.

Fritz Perls

Mental health appears as responsibility in contact: not compulsively unloading one’s chaos onto others, not invading another’s freedom, not hiding in theatrics.

Gregorio Luri, in this reading

The moral duty to be intelligent becomes adulthood: someone has to assume the burden of thinking better, transmitting a world, setting limits, and not leaving the child alone before the void.

Axis Diagnosis Risk Resulting discipline
Knowledge We know almost nothing ultimately; we only operate some scales fairly well. Mistaking technical power for ontological wisdom. Intellectual humility without fairground anti-intellectualism.
Meaning Life carries no obligatory meaning. Falling into hysterical nihilism or plastic spirituality. Giving meaning without demanding cosmic applause.
Death Death and social judgment are inevitable. Living shrunk by fear or overcompensating with excess. A sober freedom: less fear, less display, less melodrama.
Psyche Interiority works as a practical interface, not as a sacred throne. Idolizing the self or deregulating it completely. Self-observation, regulation, respect for the other, healthy contact.
Injustice Human atrocity is not a passing anomaly; it is a constant possibility of the species. Political naivety or total cynicism. Tragic lucidity: watch, contain, educate, civilize, without childish fantasies.
Childhood Children suffer when adults abdicate their role. Leaving the child trapped in the Telemachus complex: without a worthy reference, mature authority, or habitable world. Moral duty to be intelligent: an adult must take on the work of thinking better because another depends on it.
Daily practice

Maintenance question

1. Threat

What threatens my lucidity today: fear, vanity, dependency, resentment, excess, distraction, or the need for approval?

2. Integration

If I cannot eliminate it, how do I integrate it without letting it colonize my conduct or spill onto others?

3. Elimination

If I can eliminate it, why do I keep decorating it? Adult intelligence sometimes means cutting, not endlessly understanding.

Closing

An ethic without plush

This piece does not promise comfort. It says something drier: perhaps there is no accessible final ground, perhaps all interiority is interface, perhaps the world is stranger than ordinary conversation can bear. Precisely for that reason adult intelligence matters more, not less. If atrocity always was and will remain human, then the moral duty is not to dream of a clean species: it is to think better, damage less, sustain the child, and not lie too much to oneself about the cosmos.

  • There is no cosmic obligation of meaning.
  • There may still be a human obligation of lucidity.
  • That is where real adulthood begins.
