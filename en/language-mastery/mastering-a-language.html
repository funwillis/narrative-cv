Language · precision · attention

Mastering a language

Mastering a language is not talking a lot, sounding cultured, or collecting polished words like professional jewelry. It is something else: drawing precise distinctions, detecting ambiguity, shifting register when the context changes, writing without needless noise, and above all never confusing the symbol with the fact. Dijkstra reminds us that formal rigor is not a punishment but a privilege against nonsense. Krishnamurti reminds us that the word is not the thing. Together they yield a sober thesis: good language narrows the margin for absurdity, but it never replaces the reality it is trying to indicate.

Dijkstra Krishnamurti Formalism Perception
Central thesis Mastering a language means thinking more clearly without kneeling before words.

Neither emotive verbosity nor technical fetishism: enough precision to avoid nonsense, enough humility not to worship the map.

Working definition A mastered language reduces friction, error, and self-deception.

It helps us describe, ask, doubt, refine, and coordinate more effectively. When it fails, it multiplies fog, pose, and misunderstanding.

1. What it really means

Mastery is not verbal shine but discriminative power

Real mastery appears when a person can use language as an instrument of fine distinction. They can name without inflation, summarize without mutilation, qualify without turning gelatinous, and formalize when needed. They do not depend on a single register: they can speak with human precision in ordinary conversation and with technical precision when the problem demands narrower rules.

1 Distinguish

Not placing thing, opinion, metaphor, hypothesis, memory, and data in the same sack.

2 Compress

Saying much with little, without letting brevity destroy the structure of what matters.

3 Shift register

Speaking differently to a child, a colleague, a cultivated reader, or a machine without betraying the content.

4 Formalize

Knowing when a language should be tightened with definitions, syntax, or explicit rules.

The better you master a language, the less you depend on tricks, filler, and sentimental equivocation.

2. Dijkstra against fog

Why formal rigor does not impoverish thought: it protects it

In EWD 667, Dijkstra mocks, rightly, the fantasy that what feels “natural” automatically simplifies technical thinking. His sharpest point is simple: natural languages leave too much room for nonsense not to be obvious. Formal languages therefore should not be treated as a burden but as a filter: a few simple rules can expel vast amounts of confusion that ordinary speech happily disguises as depth.

Complex problem → Clear definitions → Controlled syntax → Less invisible error
Dijkstra sees Verbal looseness as risk.

The more elastic the sentence, the easier it becomes to hide confusion beneath persuasive style.

Dijkstra corrects The nostalgia of comfort.

A more “natural” interface can distribute work better, or it can double the burden on both sides. Very often it does the latter.

Dijkstra defends Useful narrowness.

A narrower language forces better thought before action. Annoying, yes. Also a fine way to prevent costly stupidity.

3. Krishnamurti against idolatry

The word serves; the word does not rule

Krishnamurti attacks the opposite vice: believing that once something has been named, it has been understood. His hammer is simple and brutal: the word is not the thing; an idea is not a fact; only what is can be transformed. If Dijkstra cleans the channel, Krishnamurti cleans consciousness of the channel. One fights imprecision; the other fights verbal hypnosis.

Perceptual correction Naming is not the same as seeing.

Labeling something “fear”, “love”, “success”, or “trauma” may orient observation, but it may also replace observation with a pre-chewed verbal package.

Psychological correction Much inner speech is self-referential noise.

When inward language spins without contact with fact, it does not clarify: it recycles the past, projects the future, and mass-produces identity.

Mature language points more accurately because it accepts its own limit: indicating is not possessing.

4. Fertile union

Enough rigor to avoid delirium; enough humility not to absolutize symbols

Putting Dijkstra and Krishnamurti together yields a rather decent criterion for almost any intellectual craft. The first reminds us that without clear form the mind becomes loquacious mud. The second reminds us that without attention to fact clear form can become an elegant idol. Full mastery does not choose one against the other; it sustains both disciplines at once.

Without Dijkstra Sensitivity becomes fog.

Everything sounds profound because nothing is defined sharply enough to fail clearly.

Without Krishnamurti Precision becomes arrogance.

The verbal system grows so neat that it forgets to check whether it still touches the reality it meant to describe.

With both Language becomes an instrument of lucidity.

More ability to coordinate, think, and refine; less temptation to confuse verbal elegance with truth.

5. How to recognize it

Practical signals of linguistic mastery

Plane Poor use Competent use Excellent use Typical risk
Ordinary conversationListening, response, tone, and context. Speaks by discharge, repeats clichés, flattens nuance. Makes oneself understood and adjusts the register to the interlocutor. Thinks while speaking, listens for real, and reformulates without caricature. Mistaking fluency for understanding.
WritingStructure, synthesis, progression. Accumulates decorative or abstract sentences without internal architecture. Orders ideas clearly, prunes redundancy, and leaves behind a testable thesis. Condenses complexity without losing tension or semantic precision. Using verbal density to hide holes in thought.
Technical or formal languageDefinitions, rules, constraints. Resists definitions and is irritated by exact syntax. Accepts formal limits when they improve coordination and error control. Can design or choose the right degree of formality for the problem. Fetishizing formalism and forgetting the world it was meant to serve.
Inner dialogueNaming states, thinking, revising. Ruminates inherited labels and identity scripts. Uses words to clarify and detect bias. Can suspend language when it obstructs and return to it when it illuminates. Confusing self-narration with living fact.
LearningReading, study, transfer. Memorizes terms without digestion. Connects concepts to examples, counterexamples, and uses. Transfers principles across domains without forcing empty analogies. Believing that remembering vocabulary is the same as knowing.
6. Short formula

So why does it matter so much?

Because nearly all adult life unfolds in symbols: contracts, promises, instructions, news, diagnoses, prayers, code, laws, and intimate narratives. Whoever does not master language is more exposed to two rather ordinary tyrannies:

  • the tyranny of other people's confusion, when others name reality for them;
  • the tyranny of their own confusion, when inward words replace observation.

Mastering a language means being able to say better, think better, and stay silent better.
