# Narrative guidelines · Stories section

## Section identity

- `Stories` is not a sub-variant of the infographics.
- The priority here is **narrative, cohesion, emotion, and transformation**.
- Philosophical theses must enter **embodied in characters, scenes, choices, losses, desire, community, and conflict**.
- Geography should be **fictional and evocative**, inspired by real places but never copied literally.
- Romance should remain **romantic and mysterious**, not sexually explicit.

## Composition rule

- Every story must feel like **one work**, not like disconnected capsules.
- It may move at high speed, but it must preserve:
  - a readable causal line
  - a central desire
  - a recognizable transformation
  - an ending, open or closed, with real emotional charge

## Minimal architecture

- Every long story should include:
  - **romance or a strong affective bond**
  - **transformation**
  - a **monomyth** or journey of change
  - **short subplots per chapter**
- Each chapter should work as roughly **1 minute of reading**:
  - if the reader stops there, it should still feel complete
  - if the reader continues, it should pull immediately toward the next chapter

## Style

- Tone reference:
  - direct
  - sober
  - quick
  - condensed
  - free of ornamental rhetoric
  - with a light aura of mystery
- Psychology:
  - **implicit in the facts**
  - do not over-explain what the character feels
  - let gestures, dialogue, silences, and actions carry the emotion
- Dialogue:
  - brief
  - precise
  - charged with friction or revelation
- The narrator may be a character, but should not sound like a lecturer dictating conclusions.
- Geography should feel inhabited rather than documented.
- Avoid real place names when the story benefits from aura or symbolic displacement.
- The introduction should suggest origins and tensions rather than declaring them in a school-like way.

## Audience

- Stories should contain ingredients that can attract both women and men:
  - intimacy
  - desire
  - moral conflict
  - adventure
  - purpose
  - risk
  - tenderness
  - loss
  - cooperation

## Romance and restraint

- Affective tension should rely on:
  - waiting
  - care
  - loyalty
  - recognition
  - shared memory
- Avoid:
  - explicit eroticization
  - overly bodily phrasing
  - scenes that break mystery or restraint
  - openly sexual tension

## Stylistic continuity

- Use `writer-profile.md` as the living reference.
- Keep memory of variants in `styles-catalog.md`.
- When a story starts from a real place, it should transmute it into fictional geography recognizable only by echo.

## Character development

- The depth of each character will depend largely on the **available length**.
- In short or medium stories, a few vivid traits are enough.
- In long stories, every important character should have:
  - a way of loving
  - a wound or limit
  - a visible decision

## Relation to the project's ideas

- Philosophical ideas must pass through:
  - bodies
  - situations
  - relationships
  - choices
- Never present a thesis as a table disguised as fiction.
- If theory appears, it should appear because the character **needs it in order to live, love, choose, or lose something**.
- Avoid direct lifts or quasi-quotes from contemporary teachers: their intuitions should be reformulated inside the main plot or the side plots.
- If an abstract ideal appears, it must land as a verifiable experience, habit, regret, care, or consequence, not as a floating slogan.

## Layout

- The layout of `Stories` must clearly differ from that of the infographics:
  - a more sober reading column
  - fewer analytical blocks
  - more textual continuity
  - visible chapters, but no dashboard aesthetic
