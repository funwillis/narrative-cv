A Love from Another Planet

If you feel like it, leave your noisiest certainties by the door for a moment. The trip is easier to enjoy that way. The planet we are heading toward is not in this solar system. Its name is Love. Sometimes it looks like a metaphor; sometimes like a future homeland. In any case, it is better approached with a little patience and with the heart less armored.

Everything began on an island of lime, salt, and deep courtyards, among people who had arrived from far away and people who had never fully left. We were two Latin American immigrants with Spanish and Italian ancestors: me, Matias Rinaldi Serra; her, Lucia Ferretti Salas. The island received us the way certain ports do: promising nothing, but leaving enough room for a life to change if one arrives willing.

Chapter 1

The woman in the blue coat

I first saw her in the library of the Old Wharf. She wore a blue coat, carried a red notebook, and turned pages with dry precision. I had gone there to return a book. She was reading The Aleph. I sat far away. Ten minutes later she looked up and said:

"If Borges were right, speaking would be indecent."

I laughed. She did not. She called me with one finger. I went. I saw her name on the notebook: Lucia. She showed me an underlined passage. Then she said:

"You put half the universe inside one word and then expect to argue calmly."

That was the beginning. Not of a theory. Of a ruin.

Chapter 2

The first fall

We began reading together every Thursday. It did not look like a date. It was one. Lucia brought Plato. I brought Borges. Sometimes neither of us opened a book. We talked about words, symbols, and the strange thing by which one person says love and another hears prison. On the fourth week she told me:

"Do not fall for me because of my mind. That would be vulgar."

I answered:

"I am already too late."

She laughed and then became serious. That seriousness was what left me defenseless.

We were not alone on those Thursdays. Sometimes the evening ended with Alma Quiroga and Dante Bellini, a couple so hardworking that complaining in front of them felt indecent. Alma, golden in skin and patience, always seemed to have solved something before the rest of us noticed the problem. Dante kept a small African drum and knew how to make the table vibrate to the rhythm of the coals whenever we gathered to eat. They almost always came with their daughter, Lia, a spark on uncertain feet who struck the floor with spoons as if she wanted to discover the beat of the world.

Chapter 3

Plato at the bar

One night we took shelter from the wind in a small bar in the Chalk Quarter. Lucia drew a ladder on a napkin. At the bottom she wrote body. At the top she wrote beauty without an owner.

"Diotima," she said. "Desire begins glued to skin. If it does not learn how to rise, it rots."

I wanted to move a little closer. She withdrew her hand.

"Not yet. Let us see whether you are confusing hunger with destiny."

I had no name for it then, but I saw the whole trouble on the table: life pushes, takes, wants; if it never learns how to rise, every love ends as appetite in elegant clothing.

Chapter 4

The priest, the atheist, and Jesus Christ

Lucia dragged me one Sunday to a soup kitchen near the Lower Market. "To read the Gospel, you need to look at its hands," she said. There an old atheist served soup as if he were repairing an engine. A young priest cleared plates. Neither of them spoke about God. Lucia whispered:

"Love is not tested when it comes easily. It is tested when you would rather give nothing."

I came home with a simple phrase: love your neighbor was not an elegant metaphor. If impulse rules, you return blow for blow. If consciousness appears, you see the blow before releasing your arm.

That night we had dinner with Mariela Pons and Bruno Vassallo. She taught pastry to girls who had dropped out of school and spoke about dough the way others speak about education: if you force too hard, you break the form; if you wait the right amount, something rises from inside. Bruno ran a kitchen at the port with the face of a man who tolerated no nonsense, yet he adjusted the position of a knife the way one might mend an injured hand. Lucia loved them because they knew how to turn simple things into a feast without making the table solemn.

Chapter 5

Time lives in the nerves

For weeks Lucia insisted on one idea. She repeated it while walking along the edge of the water, while eating, while waiting for me under damp eaves in the old center.

"Time is the infinite concatenation of our nervous system."

She said that attention, memory, and the nervous system formed a single storage system. That our biography was not pure narrative but a chain of retained impacts. That truth was therefore individual and subjective, and therefore non-transferable. I argued. She laughed.

"If you want to convince everyone, you have understood nothing."

That month I slept badly. I began to suspect that the mind was more porous than it seemed.

Chapter 6

Augustine and the stone church

We did not need Hippo. A small old church in the Valley of Bells and a long November afternoon were enough. Lucia said that if Plato had raised the ladder, Augustine had moved it into an interior cathedral. We entered when almost nobody was left. She spoke without lowering her voice:

"Tradition always canonizes an ascent. It saves it and spoils it at the same time."

That hit me. Every word preserves something and betrays something. Every theology fixes a fire so it will not go out, and in fixing it removes its air.

Chapter 7

Jung, shadow, and the multiple man

We began to fight for real when Jung appeared. I believed myself more coherent than I was. Lucia did not let a single thing pass.

"When things go well you say, 'I did it.' When things go badly you look for someone else inside yourself. Fear. Karma. Ego. A substitute always appears."

Then she added:

"An individual is what is no longer divisible. If you are wonderful, it is you. If you are unpleasant, it is you."

I got angry. She let me get angry. That night I did not walk her home. I walked alone until I ran out of excuses.

Turning by High Square I ran into Camila Ordonez and Renzo Fiore. Camila was carrying Trufa, a nervous little cat she had rescued from an empty lot and protected with almost ferocious tenderness. Renzo always smelled of bergamot or vetiver; he was one of those men who know more about perfumes than politics and therefore listen better. I told them nothing. Camila touched my shoulder and said, "Do not wear a widower's face when the girl is still alive." It did me good.

Chapter 8

It is better to walk alone

A week later she called me to the Salt Lookout. There was wind. She did not come any closer. She said:

"One tradition says: if you want a short and fast journey, walk alone; if you want a long journey, walk in company. But what matters is who walks with you."

She went quiet for a moment and finished:

"It is better to walk alone than to walk with a fool."

She looked at me to see whether I understood. She was not calling me a fool. She was telling me that much togetherness is often only fear of solitude.

"Before you join anyone," she said, "learn to sit alone. If you do not know how to be alone, company will become a great nuisance."

Chapter 9

The night we almost did not sleep

That same night she made me do something foolish and decisive. "Twenty-four hours," she said. "No reading, no phone, no music, no screens. Sit and watch." I did it in my flat in the Dry Bridge district. After three hours I wanted to run. After seven I wanted to cry. After twelve I began to see the real traffic of my own head.

"When you observe your mind," she had told me, "you will certify it yourself: this is a madman."

I went back to her the next day looking ruined. She smiled.

"Now you know the basic thing. A human being can simply be. If you wish consciously, you can evolve tonight. There is no upper line."
Chapter 10

Krishnamurti on the platform

We were saying goodbye at the Inner Harbor Station when Krishnamurti came up. Lucia said:

"Repetition kills. Once you turn truth into method, you have already lost it."

The train was late. We spoke about stillness. Not passivity. Stillness as water that stops stirring mud. Then she jumped to Chomsky and linguistics as if there were no distance between the two.

"Language gives you structure," she said. "But it does not give you truth. It gives you a powerful machine for ordering the world and also for getting lost inside it."

Then I voiced the absurd hypothesis for the first time:

"What if thoughts come from another planet?"

Lucia did not laugh. She said:

"Maybe. Once the nervous system matures, perhaps it tunes in. And perhaps language is only a relative system for translating signals."

That was where the planet Love appeared.

Chapter 11

Unconditional acceptance

The great discovery was not astronomical. It was linguistic. During an argument in Clear Port, in a cheap boarding house over the quay, Lucia struck the table and said:

"I do not like 'unconditional love.' It has produced too much guilt. I prefer 'unconditional acceptance.'"

I let her talk. She spoke fast, without ornament.

"Do not feel guilty for being unable to love. But do not try to change anyone who is not in visible trouble. That is the difference between caring and indoctrinating. Do not take anything personally. Forgive the ignorance of others. Your context may be as incomplete as theirs. That is patience."

Then I understood the knot of human conflict. Division begins when one side cannot accept the existence of the other without hatred. Without that clarity there is no community. And without conscious community there is no long voyage.

A few days later, during a meal put together by Tadeo Lujan and Nicolas Spinelli, I understood that theory needed bodies. Those two human capybaras, steady in step and decided in gaze, built catering operations as if they were organizing truces. They argued over salt, over wine temperature, over the order in which dishes should leave the kitchen, and a minute later they were shoulder to shoulder again. "For a short journey, alone," Tadeo said after hearing us. "For a long one, with whoever knows how to serve without acting like a hero," Nicolas finished. Lucia looked at me and I knew the comment was also about us.

Chapter 12

The ship

We did not achieve full noetic panhumanism. That would have been too clean for us. But enough was won to secure a democratic and financial majority for the expedition. Some brought money. Others blueprints. Others votes. Others patience. Lucia and I brought a map made of books, arguments, and sleepless nights.

I write this now from the ship. Lucia sleeps in the bunk above me. Outside there is no landscape. Only vast blackness. We do not know the outcome. We do know that everything manifested on the physical plane was first thought, and that thought may be nothing more than sound loaded with meaning. We also know this: absolutely everything bears fruit. You will harvest actions from your words and from your thoughts.

If you are reading this logbook, you already belong to the small community heading toward planet Love. Not because you believe me. Because you collaborate. Because you sit alone sometimes. Because you stop blaming your interior substitute. Because you join consciously. Amor Vincit Omnia. Not as a sticker. As work.

Mission status

The species can remain in compulsion, open itself to consciousness, or mature toward a stillness that is not for sale. The story ends here, but the voyage does not. The ship keeps moving. We keep moving. And planet Love, if it exists, will only allow itself to be touched by humble people.
