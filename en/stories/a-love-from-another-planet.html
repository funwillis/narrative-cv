A Love from Another Planet

If you feel like it, come in slowly. This notebook reads better with less hurry and less certainty. It speaks of an island, a handful of friends, and a way of living that some of us ended up calling Love for lack of a less worn-out word.

Everything began on an island of lime, salt, and deep courtyards, among people who had arrived from far away and people who had never fully left. I was Matias Rinaldi Serra. She was Lucia Ferretti Salas. The island received us the way certain ports do: promising nothing, but leaving enough room for a life to change if one arrives willing.

Chapter 1

The woman in the blue coat

I first saw her in the library of the Old Wharf. She wore a blue coat, carried a red notebook, and turned pages with dry precision. I had gone there to return a book. She was reading a volume of stories. I sat far away. Ten minutes later she looked up and said:

"If one word really tried to hold everything, speaking would be indecent."

I laughed. She did not. She called me with one finger. I went. I saw her name on the notebook: Lucia. She showed me an underlined passage. Then she said:

"You put half the universe inside one word and then expect to argue calmly."

That was the beginning. Not of a theory. Of a ruin. On my way out, Vera Quiroga, a support teacher and mother of Lia, who was shelving children's books, glanced at the book upside down in my hands and smiled as if she had already understood something before I had.

That was also the day I met Dante Bellini, who had come to measure a damp patch in the ceiling. He did not raise his voice. He knocked twice on the wall, looked at the crack, and said, "This did not break today. It has been warning for a while." Then he stayed talking to Vera about plaster, lime, and timber as if they were discussing the character of a family.

Chapter 2

Thursdays

We began seeing each other every Thursday. It did not look like a date. It was one. Sometimes we carried books. Sometimes neither of us opened them. We talked about words, symbols, and the strange thing by which one person says love and another hears prison. On the fourth week she told me:

"Do not fall for me because of my mind. That would be vulgar."

I answered:

"I am already too late."

She laughed and then became serious. That seriousness was what left me defenseless.

We were not alone on those Thursdays. Sometimes the evening ended with Vera Quiroga and Dante Bellini, who worked hard and spoke little. Vera had just started a night course in child psychology. She said she wanted to understand why some children stop asking questions precisely when they most need to. Dante lived obsessed with materials. It was enough for him to touch a railing or look at a bad joint to know whether someone had worked in haste or with respect. Lia moved from side to side with a spoon in her hand as if she had business of her own to attend to.

Lucia understood them immediately. I took longer. She saw before I did the continuity between a badly made wall, a child no one listens to, and a conversation gone wrong.

Chapter 3

The soup kitchen

One Sunday Lucia took me to a soup kitchen near the Lower Market. She had been turning over one simple idea: one becomes degraded when one learns to look sideways at other people's pain.

"Almost everyone regrets, sooner or later, not having loved better," she said.

In the soup kitchen an old atheist served soup as if he were repairing an engine. A small woman dried trays without speaking. That impressed me more than any book.

That night we had dinner with Mariela Pons, who taught pastry to girls who had dropped out of school, and Bruno Vassallo, head cook at the port and a man of few concessions. Mariela taught pastry to girls who had dropped out of school and spoke about dough the way others speak about education. If you force too much, you break it. If you wait well, something rises from inside. Bruno ran a kitchen at the port and had a clean severity about him. Before service he washed his hands and forearms with a care no one questioned, and at dusk he asked for one quiet minute by the back door. Once he mentioned a winter far from the island, with trams, public housing, and a prayer room hidden behind a supermarket. He did not explain further. He did not need to.

Mariela told us one of her students had come back to class only because someone told her she had a good hand for decorating. Lucia looked at me as if she wanted to say that whole lives change in just that way: by one exact gesture at the right time.

Chapter 4

What warns you

For weeks Lucia insisted on one idea. She repeated it while walking along the edge of the water, while eating, while waiting for me under damp eaves in the old center.

"We become what we remember and what we repeat."

She said attention, memory, and the body form a single habit. Then she mentioned Emerson in passing: a person is his memory just as he is his food. After that she said no truth passes intact from one person to another. I argued. She laughed.

"If you want to convince everyone, you have understood nothing."

That month I slept badly. I began to suspect that the mind was more porous than it seemed. Lucia kept showing up with the same red notebook, as if everything we had said were not separate pieces but one single thing we still did not know how to name properly.

Vera began leaving tiny notes in the margins of borrowed books: "naming does not calm," "sometimes listening teaches more than correcting." Dante, by contrast, spoke of bricks, beams, and mixes. He said a material always warns before it gives way. "People do too," he added one afternoon without looking at me. That was how I knew he had seen my disorder before I had.

Chapter 5

The cathedral

We did not need Hippo. A cathedral by the port and a long November afternoon were enough. Lucia said she distrusted ideas that never pass through guilt, compassion, and the night. We entered when almost nobody was left. She spoke without lowering her voice:

"Every tradition saves something and damages something. What matters is not using it to stop looking."

That hit me because it was still tied to everything before it. Every word preserves something and betrays something. Every idea that tries to protect a fire risks suffocating it.

When we came out, Bruno was smoking on the opposite corner, waiting for Mariela after her shift. She carried a small box of crooked sponge cakes. "These are the ones that never make it to the counter," she said. It struck me as a decent way to love the world.

That was also the night I truly argued with Lucia. Not over a grand idea. Over one of my small meannesses. She let me talk until I heard myself alone. Then she said, "You always want to be right one minute longer than necessary." I did not walk her home.

Chapter 6

The truce

I walked alone as far as High Square. There I ran into Camila Ordonez, who took in stray animals and joked in a dry way, and Renzo Fiore, a perfumer by trade and a listener by vocation. Camila had Mist pressed against her coat. Renzo smelled of clean soap and a bitter note I could not name. I told them nothing, but Camila said:

"Do not wear the face of an ending over an argument that arrived on time."

The next day Lucia asked me to meet her at the Inner Harbor Station. She said sometimes a truce well lived teaches more than a badly defended lifetime.

"Not everything gets fixed by talking more," she said. "Sometimes it is enough to stop making it worse."
"One tradition says: if you want a short and fast journey, walk alone; if you want a long journey, walk in company. But what matters is who walks with you."

That same night she made me do something foolish and decisive. "Twenty-four hours," she said. "No reading, no phone, no music, no screens. Sit and watch." I did it in my flat in the Dry Bridge district. After three hours I wanted to run. After seven I wanted to cry. After twelve I began to see the real traffic of my own head.

"When you observe your mind," she had told me, "you will certify it yourself: this is a madman."

I went back to her the next day looking ruined. She smiled.

"Now you know the basic thing. A human being can simply be. If you wish consciously, you can evolve tonight. There is no upper line."

When I got home there was a bag hanging from the doorknob. Mariela had left dry sweet bread for dipping in coffee and a note from Bruno: "No one thinks well with a noisy stomach." Sometimes friendship arrives with flour.

Chapter 7

Acceptance

I saw Lucia again two days later in a cheap boarding house in Clear Port where Tadeo Lujan and Nicolas Spinelli, partners in a small catering business and friends who argue while working, were closing a delayed catering job. They argued about the order in which dishes should go out and a minute later they were working together again. That was where I finally understood what Lucia had been trying to tell me from the beginning.

"Do not turn love into an impossible obligation," she said. "Acceptance already changes a great deal."

She explained that for weeks she had been correcting a word inside herself. She did not want to speak of unconditional love. It sounded like a trap, a guilt machine. She preferred acceptance, attention, and shared responsibility. I thought of the soup kitchen, the cathedral, Dante listening to a wall, Bruno asking for one clean minute before service, Mariela saving a student with one sentence.

Camila arrived late because Mist had hidden inside a crate of glasses. Vera came with a notebook full of notes on attachment and learning. During a break Dante showed me an old beam recovered from a demolition and said, "This still holds because no one loads it where it was already bent." For the first time I understood everything without needing to argue.

Chapter 8

A better life

We did not reach a better life completely. That would have been too clean for us. But we did achieve something less spectacular and more serious: we began to live in a way that would not shame us when reviewed at night.

Lucia and I are still together. Not perfectly. Attentively. Vera keeps writing about children who need less correction and more presence. Dante now restores old houses and has become unbearable about badly mixed mortars. Mariela teaches and still brings what never makes it to the counter. Bruno cooks as if he were still answering an old debt. Renzo keeps chasing the exact perfume of a good farewell. Camila takes care of Mist and laughs at all of us when we become too solemn. Tadeo and Nicolas still argue before every service and work together afterward.

If we keep calling that way of life Love, it is out of economy. It was the shortest word we found for attention, acceptance, courage, patience, and shared responsibility. Not as a slogan. As a discipline against regret.

Final note

The species can remain in compulsion, open itself to consciousness, or mature toward a stillness that is not for sale. The story ends here, but the work does not. We keep moving. And that way of living we kept calling Love only has one serious test: to live so that regret arrives less late.
