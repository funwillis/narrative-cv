Specialization or whole mind

The question is not whether specialization is useful. Of course it is. The question is what happens when a person is reduced to one function, one tool, one corner of themselves. That is where the banausos appears: someone very useful inside their furrow and increasingly clumsy outside it.

Thesis Specialization solves tasks. Interdisciplinarity keeps the task from devouring the person.

That is exactly where Krishnamurti pushes: education not as training for fitting in, but as the awakening of a whole, attentive, unfragmented mind.

Conceptual pivot Banausos is not only a manual trade.

It is a cultural warning: when doing narrows seeing, technical expertise rises while overall intelligence shrinks.

The basic contrast

Two models of human formation

One sharpens a point. The other organizes an ecosystem of capacities. The first can produce brilliant yet dependent experts. The second seeks people able to connect, understand context, and avoid getting trapped in the automatism of their role.

Specialized learning or work

It maximizes depth, repetition, technical mastery, and local performance. Its risk appears when the person confuses function with identity.

The danger point

When expertise degenerates into banausos

The banausos embodies enclosed skill. It knows how to operate one part of the world, but has lost living relation with the whole. It functions, produces, executes, but no longer looks. That is the hard critique: an education designed only for function ends up manufacturing effective yet mentally narrow people.

1. Local competence

The person learns a concrete technique, gains precision, and becomes valuable in one defined segment of the process.

2. Functional enclosure

Everything that does not improve immediate performance starts to seem secondary: art, ethics, context, politics, interiority.

3. Human narrowing

One ends up knowing a lot about one thing and little about oneself, the other, and the system on which one's own task depends.

The pedagogical way out

Krishnamurti: education is not the production of parts

In Jiddu Krishnamurti’s pedagogical model, valid education does not consist in adapting the student to a social or professional machine. It consists in awakening observation, inner freedom, and understanding of conditioning. A fragmented mind can be competent; an attentive mind can also be intelligent.

Attention before training

The student learns to observe how they think, what they fear, what they imitate, and what they desire.

Totality before function

Formation includes relationship, sensitivity, nature, conflict, creativity, and self-knowledge.

Freedom before conformity

The goal is not to produce polished gears, but human beings not trapped by the mold.

What changes when the axis is interdisciplinary

Interdisciplinarity does not deny depth. It relocates it. Instead of digging one well and living inside it, it allows bridges to be drawn between wells: science with ethics, technique with language, work with consciousness, problem with context.

Connects levels

It joins detail and system, operation and meaning, craft and society.

Reduces blindness

It exposes the biases of one’s own field and forces the obvious to be translated for others.

Protects the mind

It prevents identity from being hijacked by a single professional label.

The synthesis

This is not about abolishing specialists. It is about not breeding banausoi.

The key point is this: a society needs experts, but a worthy education cannot limit itself to producing them. If it forms only specialists, it creates efficiency without vision. If it integrates depth with interdisciplinarity, attention, and self-knowledge, then it forms people able to work without becoming prisoners of their work. That is where Krishnamurti comes in with the knife: function matters, yes; but a fragmented mind, however brilliant, is still broken.
